# Micro Gear Motor Selection Guide

Selecting the right micro gear motor is critical for compact motion systems. A small motor alone may not provide enough output torque at the required speed, so engineers often use a micro gear motor, miniature planetary gearbox, or custom motor gearbox assembly to achieve stable low-speed motion in limited installation space.

SigGear provides micro drive solutions as small as 6mm, as well as 6-42mm miniature and compact planetary gear reducers for micro robotics, robotic grippers, medical devices, laboratory automation, optical equipment, precision instruments, pumps, valves, smart hardware, and compact automation systems.

This guide helps engineers understand how to choose a suitable micro gear motor or miniature gearbox based on size, torque, speed, gear ratio, voltage, shaft design, mounting method, noise, duty cycle, and customization requirements.

## What Is a Micro Gear Motor?

A micro gear motor is a compact motor combined with a small gearbox. The gearbox reduces motor speed and increases output torque, making it suitable for small mechanisms that require controlled movement, compact size, and stable output.

Micro gear motors are commonly used in:

* Micro robotics
* Robotic grippers
* Medical devices
* Diagnostic equipment
* Laboratory automation
* Optical adjustment systems
* Precision instruments
* Smart hardware
* Small pumps
* Valve control systems
* Miniature actuators
* Compact automation modules

## Why Micro Gear Motor Selection Matters

A micro gear motor is often installed in a very limited space. If the motor or gearbox is not selected correctly, the final product may have insufficient torque, unstable speed, excessive noise, overheating, short lifetime, poor positioning, or difficult mechanical integration.

A suitable micro gear motor helps improve:

* Compact product design
* Stable low-speed motion
* Output torque
* Gripping or driving force
* Motion repeatability
* Noise control
* Mechanical reliability
* Prototype development speed
* Custom mechanism integration

## Step-by-Step Micro Gear Motor Selection Process

### Step 1: Define the Application

Start with the actual mechanism.

Useful questions:

* Is it a robotic gripper?
* Is it a medical device?
* Is it a pump or valve?
* Is it a micro robot?
* Is it an optical adjustment mechanism?
* Is it a compact actuator?
* Is it a prototype or mass production product?

Different applications may need different motor types, gear ratios, shaft designs, and noise levels.

## Step 2: Confirm Size Limit

Micro drive selection usually starts with mechanical space.

Confirm:

* Maximum outer diameter
* Maximum total length
* Shaft length
* Shaft direction
* Wire outlet direction
* Mounting screw position
* Available space for encoder or brake
* Allowed weight

For ultra-compact products, SigGear can support micro drive solutions as small as 6mm. For higher torque compact applications, SigGear can also support 6-42mm planetary gear reducers.

## Step 3: Confirm Required Output Torque

Output torque determines whether the motor can move the mechanism reliably.

Engineers should confirm:

* Required continuous torque
* Peak torque
* Starting torque
* Holding torque
* Safety factor
* Friction load
* Object weight
* Mechanism efficiency
* Load change during operation

For robotic grippers, torque affects gripping force.
For pumps and valves, torque affects reliable opening, closing, or fluid control.
For optical and medical equipment, torque affects stable adjustment and repeated movement.

## Step 4: Confirm Required Output Speed

Micro motors often rotate at high speed, while the final mechanism needs lower speed. The gearbox reduces speed and increases torque.

Confirm:

* Target output speed
* Motor speed range
* Required motion time
* Start-stop frequency
* Acceleration and deceleration needs
* Low-speed smoothness requirement

A higher gear ratio usually provides more output torque but lower output speed.

## Step 5: Choose Gear Ratio

Gear ratio is one of the most important parameters in micro gear motor selection.

| Gear Ratio Choice | Typical Result                                                 |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Lower ratio       | Higher output speed, lower torque multiplication               |
| Medium ratio      | Balanced speed and torque                                      |
| Higher ratio      | Higher output torque, lower output speed                       |
| Very high ratio   | More torque, but possible lower efficiency and slower response |

The best gear ratio depends on motor speed, required output speed, torque requirement, efficiency, noise, and control response.

## Step 6: Choose Motor Type

Different motor types are suitable for different control and lifetime requirements.

| Motor Type      | Suitable Use                                                    |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| DC motor        | Simple control, compact products, smart hardware, pumps, valves |
| Brushless motor | Longer life, better control, medical devices, lab automation    |
| Stepper motor   | Positioning applications, instruments, automation modules       |
| Servo motor     | Higher control requirement, precision motion systems            |
| Custom motor    | Application-specific compact drive solution                     |

SigGear can help evaluate motor matching according to speed, torque, voltage, control method, and mechanical interface.

## Step 7: Check Shaft and Mounting Design

Micro gear motors often need custom output interfaces.

Common shaft options include:

* Round shaft
* D-shaft
* Flat shaft
* Gear shaft
* Threaded shaft
* Spline shaft
* Custom shaft length
* Custom shaft diameter
* Custom output gear

Mounting options may include screw holes, flange, bracket, housing interface, or custom mechanical structure.

A custom shaft or mounting interface can reduce extra adapters and improve assembly efficiency.

## Step 8: Evaluate Noise and Lifetime

Noise and lifetime are especially important in medical devices, laboratory equipment, smart hardware, service robots, and indoor applications.

Check:

* Gearbox noise under load
* Motor noise
* Vibration
* Heat generation
* Duty cycle
* Load profile
* Operating temperature
* Required lifetime
* Lubrication and material requirements

Prototype testing is recommended because small mechanisms are sensitive to friction, alignment, load variation, and assembly tolerance.
