# Robot Gripper Gear Motors and Dexterous Robotic Hand Actuators

## Application Overview

Robotic grippers, dexterous robotic hands, tendon-driven fingers and compact end-effectors require miniature actuator systems that combine small size, low weight, controllable motion and reliable transmission performance.

A robot gripper gear motor or dexterous hand actuator should be selected according to the complete finger or gripper mechanism, including required force, speed, stroke, tendon routing, spool diameter, duty cycle, noise requirement and installation envelope.

SigGear supports compact motion solutions for robotic hands and grippers, including micro gear motors, miniature planetary gearbox solutions and motor-reducer integration evaluation.

This page is intended for early-stage actuator selection and engineering communication. Final selection requires confirmation of the approved configuration, drawing, motor matching, load condition and application duty cycle.

## Key Selection Factors for Robotic Grippers

| Requirement | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Gripping force | Determines the output torque and mechanical advantage required from the actuator. |
| Finger mechanism | Tendon drive, linkage, rack, screw or direct-drive mechanisms create different load requirements. |
| Speed requirement | Affects opening and closing time, grasping response and motor selection. |
| Size and weight | Critical for dexterous hands and lightweight end-effectors. |
| Backdrivability | May be required for safe human interaction or force-sensitive applications. |
| Noise level | Important for medical, research, service and human-facing robots. |
| Service life | Depends on cycles, load, lubrication, temperature and mechanical design. |
| Radial and axial loads | Important when tendon spools, pulleys or offset mechanisms create side loads. |
| Encoder and control | Determines position control, force estimation and repeatability. |

## Dexterous Robotic Hand Applications

Dexterous hands often require multiple compact actuators working together. Each finger joint may have different requirements depending on:

- Finger length and linkage geometry
- Tendon routing method
- Spool diameter
- Required fingertip force
- Joint range of motion
- Desired grasp speed
- Human interaction requirements
- Weight distribution across the hand

A small actuator that works for a fingertip mechanism may not be suitable for a wrist, palm or high-load grasping axis.

## Tendon-Driven Robotic Hand Selection

For tendon-driven hands, provide:

- Tendon tension requirement
- Spool diameter
- Required tendon travel
- Maximum radial load on the spool shaft
- Desired grasping speed
- Continuous and peak operating cycle
- Required backdrivability
- Noise and vibration requirement

The actuator should be evaluated as a complete mechanical system, including motor, gearbox, spool, bearings, tendon path and controller.

## Low Noise and Precision Motion Review

Low-noise robotic hand applications require review of the complete mechanism, not only the gearbox.

Important factors include:

- Gearbox selection
- Motor operating point
- Bearing condition
- Mechanical preload
- Housing stiffness
- Assembly tolerance
- Controller tuning
- Required operating speed

Noise level, lifetime and repeatability targets should be confirmed through the application test condition.

## Backdrivability and Safety Requirements

Some robotic hands require backdrivability for safe human interaction, while other applications require holding force and position retention.

Confirm:

- Whether manual movement without power is required
- Required holding torque
- Whether a brake is required
- Whether compliance or force sensing is used
- Whether the actuator must resist external impact

Backdrivability cannot be assumed from gearbox type alone and requires evaluation of the complete actuator design.
